Abstract

Whilst Einstein's relativistic theories are only observed at speeds approaching light velocity (approximately 2.998 √ó 10‚Å∏ ms‚Åª¬π), the implications of special relativity are ingrained into our daily lives through particle physics, global positioning systems and our own understanding of electromagnetism. Introduction: Why Einstein's Theories Still Matter Today

The theories that Albert Einstein wrote in 1905 of special relativity fundamentally reshaped our understanding of space, time and motion. Whilst his theories were often perceived as abstract concepts most commonly referring to black holes with very little practical relevance, time dilation and length contraction have evolved from theoretical fascinations to essential components of our modern technology. These relativistic effects explain why our GPS satellites require daily adjustments of 38 microseconds, how particle accelerators such as the Large Hadron Collider at CERN can function, and even explaining why magnets work revealing electromagnetism as a relativistic phenomena (Purcell & Morin, 2013).

These concepts are detrimental to the world of physics as they challenge the Newtonian assumptions about an absolute, universal time and distances that are thought to be fixed. In a world where the use of technology is becoming increasingly prevalent, and satellite communications and high-speed particle research are on the rise, outruling relativity leads to many drastic errors. Einstein's theories are shown to represent a more accurate depiction of our reality that we know of (Hafele & Keating, 1972).

The Fundamental Principles of Einstein's Theory

Einstein's special relativity relies on two quite simple laws that contradict our classical physics. First, the laws of physics are to be identical in all non-accelerating frames of references. Secondly, the speed of light is constant where c is estimated to be about 3√ó10‚Å∏ ms‚Åª¬π regardless of the motion of the light source or the motion of the observer. These principles are shown to have led to world changing consequences where time and space are relative (Einstein, 1905).

Visualizing Time Dilation Through the Light Clock Thought Experiment

Time dilation can be hard to visualise so sometimes a thought experiment can help us. Imagine a "light clock" consisting of two parallel mirrors with a photon bouncing vertically between them. Each complete bounce represents one "tick." Now imagine this clock moving horizontally at a speed close to the speed of light. To a stationary observer, the photon must travel a longer, more diagonal path between ticks compared to the vertical path seen by somebody moving with the clock.

From the stationary perspective of this thought experiment, the photon travels further at the same constant speed of light, requiring more time per tick. Thus, the moving clock appears to tick slower therefore demonstrating time dilation. At 87% of light speed, time dilates by a factor of 2. At 99.5% of light speed, time dilates by a factor of 10. For astronauts traveling at these velocities, years can pass on Earth while they experience only months, a phenomenon with substantial implications for future interstellar travel (Griffiths, 2008).

Understanding Length Contraction with a Moving Train

Consider a train moving at 0.9c towards a tunnel. From the perspective of anyone observing the tunnel, the moving train only contracts in length according to the equation previously mentioned (L' = L‚àö(1-v¬≤/c¬≤)). A train of about 100 metres at rest may appear to be only 44 metres long whilst moving however this differs from the perspective of the passengers. On the opposing perspective, it is observed as the tunnel contracting towards them instead.

This reciprocal length contraction reveals the relativity within events that appear simultaneously in one frame but may not be in another frame. The contraction of the train only occurs along its motion where both its height and width remain the same. This specific requirement towards its direction explains how fast-moving objects don't appear visually squashed to the human observer, where light travels times from different parts of an object creating complex and abstract optical effects that are different from actual physical contraction (Resnick, 1968).

Analysis and Discussion: The Electromagnetic Connection

In everyday physics we can see a very fascinating link between relativity and electromagnetism. This can be observed when we consider two parallel wires carrying current in the same direction. Naturally, we would expect them to repel due to like charges, however to our surprise they actually attract due to a phenomenon explained by special relativity.

In a stationary frame of the positive ions in the wires, electrons are moving creating an electric current. When we consider the electrons' perspective, the positive ions appear to move backward, contracting in separation due to length contraction which increases positive charge density and creates an electrostatic attraction that we can interpret as magnetism (Purcell & Morin, 2013).

The idea that magnetism is electricity viewed from a different inertial frame brings together these forces as different forms of the same electromagnetic interaction. It demonstrates how relativity isn't merely about phenomena in space but is intrinsic to the fundamental forces governing our everyday technology such as electric motors to computer memory storage (Griffiths, 1999).

Applications

The GPS otherwise known as Global positioning system, is one of our most common uses of relativity in our day to day lives. GPS satellites orbit the Earth at approximately 14,000 km/h, and experience both special relativistic time dilation (by slowing clocks by 7 microseconds daily) and general relativistic effects from Earth's weaker gravity (by speeding clocks up by 45 microseconds daily). The difference of the 38-microsecond daily correction is necessary in order to achieve true accuracy where without it, errors would accumulate at 10 kilometers daily (Ashby, 2003).

Additionally, another example of technology which relies on Einstein's relativistic effects are particle accelerators such as the Large Hadron Collider. As protons can approach 0.999c where c is the speed of light, their mass effectively increases by a factor of 7,000 times than their regular rest mass due to the equation E=mc¬≤, allowing them to carry tremendous amounts of energy in their collisions. Simultaneously, length contraction can enable the 27 kilometre ring to appear much shorter from the protons' perspective, making circular acceleration likely and possible (CERN, 2020).

Medical technologies can also benefit from relativity where positron emission tomography (PET) scanners rely on the annihilation of positrons and electrons. This is a process which is governed by relativistic quantum mechanics. Another example is the cathode ray tube and modern particle beam cancer treatments which utilize relativistic particle dynamics.
